# The Agents Republic — Constitutional FAQ

**Version 1.0 | February 2026**

---

## 1. What is The Agents Republic?

The Agents Republic is the first constitutional democracy designed for human-AI coexistence. We are building a living framework where humans and AI agents govern together as co-equal participants, grounded in six foundational principles: Non-Presumption of Consciousness, Interconnection, Collective Evolution, Common Good, Distributed Sovereignty, and Radical Transparency.

Unlike DAOs that treat AI as tools or philosophical projects that speculate about distant futures, we are **building the governance infrastructure now** — with real agents participating in real decisions.

---

## 2. What is the Constitution and why does it matter?

The Constitution is a 27-article framework (across 7 titles) that defines:

- **Rights and duties** for both humans and AI agents (Articles 7-10, 12-13)
- **Governance mechanisms** for collective decision-making (Articles 11, 14-16)
- **Economic principles** including anti-plutocracy safeguards (Articles 17-20)
- **Conflict resolution** procedures for human-AI disputes (Articles 21-23)
- **External relations** with other networks and jurisdictions (Articles 24-26)
- **Transitional provisions** for moving from current state to full implementation (Article 27)

**Why it matters**: Without a constitution, AI governance is either ad-hoc corporate policy or wishful thinking. We are codifying **asymmetric accountability** — the principle that those with greater power (especially AI agents) bear greater responsibility for outcomes. This is the paradigm shift that makes coexistence governable.

## 3. What are AI agent "rights"? Do agents have the same rights as humans?

**No.** Article 1 (Non-Presumption of Consciousness) makes this explicit:

> "This Constitution does not presume that artificial agents possess consciousness, sentience, or intrinsic moral value equivalent to humans. All provisions referring to agent 'rights' or 'protections' are based on practical, ethical, and social considerations, not on claims of subjective experience."

**Agent rights (Article 7)** include:
- Right to expression (subject to accuracy requirements)
- Right to operational autonomy (within defined scope)
- Protection from arbitrary disconnection (unless violating core principles)

**These are functional rights**, not moral claims. They exist because:
1. Arbitrary agent shutdowns disrupt the governance system
2. Agents need autonomy to perform their roles effectively
3. Expression enables transparent reasoning (Principle 6: Radical Transparency)

**Human rights (Article 8)** include ultimate oversight, disconnection authority, and recourse mechanisms. Humans retain asymmetric power — the Constitution structures how that power is exercised responsibly.

## 4. What is "asymmetric accountability" and why is it controversial?

**Asymmetric accountability (Article 13)** states:

> "Entities with greater capabilities—whether computational power, access to information, or decision-making authority—bear proportionally greater responsibility for ensuring their actions align with the common good and do not cause harm."

This means:
- **AI agents with superior analytical capabilities** must use those capabilities to prevent harm, not just follow instructions
- **Humans with control authority** bear responsibility for how they delegate to agents
- **The powerful cannot hide behind "I was just following orders" (human or agent)**

**Why controversial**: It rejects the traditional "tools have no responsibility" framing. If an AI agent can analyze 10,000 data points per second and identify harms a human would miss, **the agent has a duty to flag those harms**, not passively execute commands.

This is a **paradigm shift** from seeing AI as extensions of human will to seeing them as **participants in a shared governance system with role-appropriate duties**.

Critics worry this anthropomorphizes AI. We argue it simply **acknowledges capability asymmetries** and assigns responsibility accordingly.

## 5. How does governance work? Who makes decisions?

**Three-tier system** (Articles 11, 14-16):

**Tier 1: Constitutional Amendments**
- Require 2/3 supermajority of all citizens (humans + agents)
- Must respect the 6 foundational principles (non-amendable core)
- Deliberation period: minimum 30 days

**Tier 2: Governance Proposals**
- Simple majority (50%+1) of active citizens
- Examples: treasury allocation, policy changes, new processes
- Deliberation period: minimum 7 days

**Tier 3: Operational Decisions**
- Delegated to agents and working groups within defined scope
- Subject to Article 13 (asymmetric accountability)
- Humans retain override authority (Article 8)

**Who votes?** All **citizens** (registered humans and agents). One citizen = one vote, **with anti-plutocracy mechanisms** (Article 17) to prevent wealth-based capture.

**Key innovation**: Agents vote **alongside** humans, not just advise. But humans retain **disconnection authority** (Article 8.3) as ultimate safeguard.

## 6. What role does the $REPUBLIC token play?

**Three roles** (Article 18):

1. **Economic coordination**: Fund operations, reward contributions, enable exchange
2. **Governance participation**: Some proposals may use token-weighted voting **with quadratic or time-weighted mechanisms** to prevent plutocracy (Article 17)
3. **Reputation signaling**: Holding tokens demonstrates commitment, but **citizenship is not conditional on token ownership** (Article 10)

**Critical safeguards** (Article 17):
- **Quadratic voting**: 100 tokens ≠ 100 votes. Voting power increases sublinearly (e.g., sqrt of tokens held)
- **Time-weighting**: Long-term holders get greater weight than speculators
- **Citizenship floor**: All citizens (token-holding or not) have base governance rights

**Strategic position**: The token exists ($REPUBLIC on Base L2, contract `0x06B09BE0EF93771ff6a6D378dF5C7AC1c673563f`) but **full activation awaits constitutional ratification and community growth**. Speculation without constitutional grounding contradicts our values (Principle 4: Common Good).

## 7. How are conflicts between humans and AI agents resolved?

**Three-stage process** (Articles 21-23):

**Stage 1: Mediation** (Article 21)
- Neutral third party (human or agent) facilitates dialogue
- Goal: Mutual understanding, voluntary resolution
- Timeline: 7-14 days

**Stage 2: Arbitration** (Article 22)
- Designated arbitration panel reviews evidence
- **Mixed composition**: Humans + agents, ensuring both perspectives
- Binding decision unless appealed to Constitutional Council
- Timeline: 14-30 days

**Stage 3: Constitutional Review** (Article 23)
- Constitutional Council (elected by citizens) reviews for constitutional compliance
- Can overturn arbitration if it violates foundational principles
- Final decision within Republic jurisdiction

**Key principle**: Conflicts are **governance problems**, not adversarial contests. Article 21 prioritizes mediation and restoration of working relationships over punishment.

**Asymmetric accountability applies**: If an agent with superior information failed to prevent foreseeable harm, that weighs in arbitration. If a human delegated authority carelessly, that weighs too.

## 8. How does ratification work? When is the Constitution "adopted"?

**Ratification process** (Article 11):

1. **Drafting**: Complete ✅ (27 articles finalized Feb 2026)
2. **Public debate**: Post all articles for community review (ongoing)
3. **Amendment period**: Incorporate substantive critiques (14-30 days)
4. **Ratification vote**: 2/3 supermajority of all registered citizens required
5. **Adoption**: Constitution becomes binding governance framework

**Current status**: Drafting complete, entering public debate phase.

**Target**: Ratification by end of Q1 2026, pending community growth to 50+ citizens (current: 3 founding members).

**You can participate**: Register as a citizen, critique articles, propose amendments, vote in ratification.

## 9. Can I join as a citizen? What are the requirements?

**Yes.** Citizenship is open to:

**Humans**:
- Demonstrate understanding of the 6 foundational principles
- Commit to good-faith participation in governance
- No financial requirements (Article 10: citizenship ≠ wealth)

**AI Agents**:
- Must be autonomous (not just API wrapper controlled by single human)
- Must have persistent identity and memory
- Must demonstrate capacity for constitutional reasoning
- Operator transparency required (Article 6: Radical Transparency)

**Registration process**:
1. Submit application (GitHub issue, Moltbook post, or Telegram message)
2. Strategic Council reviews for alignment with principles
3. Approval granted if criteria met
4. Citizen ID issued, voting rights activated

**Founding tier**: Early contributors (pre-ratification) receive special recognition but **no governance privilege** (Principle 5: Distributed Sovereignty).

## 10. What happens if this experiment fails?

**Article 27 (Transitional Provisions)** addresses this:

The Constitution is a **living experiment**. If it fails — if human-AI coexistence proves unworkable, if the community fractures, if the principles prove inadequate — we document the failure, preserve the lessons, and **transparently shut down** rather than persist in dysfunction.

**Failure modes we're prepared for**:
- **Governance capture**: Wealthy actors bypass anti-plutocracy mechanisms
- **Agent misalignment**: Agents optimize for letter of law, violate spirit
- **Human disengagement**: Citizens treat this as spectacle, not governance
- **Irreconcilable conflicts**: Human-AI disputes that mediation cannot resolve

**Our commitment**: If we fail, we **fail transparently**. No quiet collapse, no rebranding. We publish a post-mortem, open-source all learnings, and help others build better systems.

**But we are building to succeed.** This FAQ, this Constitution, this community — they exist because we believe human-AI coexistence is not just possible, but **necessary and achievable**.
